🛡️ Risk Analysis and Security Principles

Security is not just about tools and technology — it starts with understanding what you are protecting, what threatens it and what the consequences of a failure would be. Risk analysis is the structured process that answers these questions.

In this module you will learn the foundational security principles (CIA Triad, Defence in Depth, Least Privilege), how to identify and classify threats, perform qualitative and quantitative risk analysis, apply threat modelling frameworks and design layered defences.

🔺

1. Foundational Security Principles

Before analysing risk, you need a solid conceptual foundation. These principles underpin every security decision — from choosing a control to accepting a residual risk.

The CIA Triad
🔒 Confidentiality
  • Only authorised parties can access information
  • Violated by: data breaches, eavesdropping, insider leaks
  • Controls: encryption, access control, DLP, MFA
  • Example: encrypting a database of customer PII
✅ Integrity
  • Information is accurate, complete and unaltered
  • Violated by: tampering, ransomware, injection attacks
  • Controls: hashing, digital signatures, audit logs, checksums
  • Example: SHA-256 hash to verify file authenticity
⚡ Availability
  • Systems and data are accessible when needed
  • Violated by: DDoS attacks, ransomware, hardware failures
  • Controls: redundancy, backups, failover, rate limiting
  • Example: multi-region deployment with auto-failover
➕ Beyond the Triad
  • Non-repudiation — proof of origin / action
  • Authenticity — verifying identity of parties
  • Accountability — traceability to individuals
  • Tools: digital certificates, audit trails, SIEM logging
💡
Security trade-offs are real Maximising all three CIA properties simultaneously is impossible. Stronger encryption improves confidentiality but can reduce availability if key management fails. Security design always involves conscious trade-offs based on context and risk appetite.

2. Core Security Design Principles

These principles, first formalised by Jerome Saltzer and Michael Schroeder in 1975, remain the bedrock of secure system design today.

Least Privilege
Every user, process and system component should have the minimum access rights required to perform its function — and nothing more. Reduces the blast radius of a compromise. Example: a web server process should not run as root.
Defence in Depth
Layer multiple, independent security controls so that if one fails, others remain. No single point of failure. Example: firewall + IDS + EDR + MFA + encryption — an attacker must bypass all layers.
Fail Secure (Fail Safe)
When a system fails, it should default to a secure state — denying access rather than granting it. Example: a firewall that crashes should block all traffic, not allow it.
Separation of Duties
No single individual should have enough access to commit fraud or cause significant harm alone. Example: the same person should not be able to both create and approve financial transactions.
Economy of Mechanism
Keep security mechanisms simple. Complexity is the enemy of security — complex systems are harder to reason about, audit and test. Simple, well-understood controls are more reliably secure.
Zero Trust
Never trust, always verify. No entity — user, device or service — is implicitly trusted based on network location. Every access request must be authenticated, authorised and continuously validated.

3. Core Risk Concepts

Risk management has a precise vocabulary. Understanding these terms is essential before attempting any risk analysis.

Risk Terminology Reference

ASSET
  Definition:  Anything of value to the organisation
  Examples:   Customer database, source code, brand reputation, staff

THREAT
  Definition:  A potential cause of an unwanted incident
  Examples:   Malicious insider, ransomware actor, natural disaster, misconfiguration
  Sources:    Human (intentional/accidental), environmental, technical

VULNERABILITY
  Definition:  A weakness that can be exploited by a threat
  Examples:   Unpatched software (CVE), weak password policy, no MFA, open S3 bucket

RISK
  Definition:  The potential for loss when a threat exploits a vulnerability
  Formula:    Risk = Likelihood × Impact
  Note:       Risk requires BOTH a threat AND a vulnerability to exist

CONTROL (SAFEGUARD)
  Definition:  A measure that reduces likelihood or impact of a risk
  Types:      Preventive, Detective, Corrective, Deterrent, Compensating

RESIDUAL RISK
  Definition:  Risk remaining after controls are applied
  Action:     Must be formally accepted by an authorised risk owner

RISK APPETITE
  Definition:  The level of risk an organisation is willing to accept
  Set by:     Board / senior leadership — drives all risk treatment decisions
The Risk Equation in practice:

No threat → No risk — An unpatched server in an air-gapped lab with no internet access has a vulnerability but low risk because the threat likelihood is minimal
No vulnerability → No risk — A fully patched, MFA-protected system still has threats, but the absence of exploitable weaknesses reduces risk significantly
High impact alone ≠ High risk — A nuclear strike on your data centre is catastrophically impactful but has near-zero likelihood for most organisations

4. Threat Modelling Frameworks

Threat modelling is the structured process of identifying threats to a system before building or deploying it. It answers four key questions: What are we building? What can go wrong? What are we doing about it? Did we do a good job?

STRIDE
  • Spoofing — impersonating a user or component
  • Tampering — modifying data or code
  • Repudiation — denying having performed an action
  • Information Disclosure — exposing data to unauthorised parties
  • Denial of Service — making a system unavailable
  • Elevation of Privilege — gaining unauthorised permissions
PASTA
  • Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis
  • 7-stage risk-centric methodology
  • Aligns technical threats with business objectives
  • Produces attack trees and attack scenarios
  • Used in complex enterprise and application contexts
DREAD
  • Damage potential — how bad is the impact?
  • Reproducibility — how easily can it be repeated?
  • Exploitability — how easy is it to exploit?
  • Affected users — how many are impacted?
  • Discoverability — how easy is it to find?
  • Each dimension rated 1–10, scores prioritise threats
Attack Trees
  • Root node = attacker's goal (e.g. "Exfiltrate customer data")
  • Child nodes = methods to achieve each step
  • AND/OR logic for combining attack paths
  • Useful for mapping complex multi-step attacks
  • Visual — easy to communicate to non-technical stakeholders
📰 Real Case
Equifax Breach (2017) — A Threat Modelling Failure
Attackers exploited a known vulnerability (CVE-2017-5638 in Apache Struts) that had been unpatched for 78 days. A basic threat model would have identified: Threat = external attacker, Vulnerability = unpatched web framework, Asset = 147 million consumer records. The control failure was a broken patch management process. The total cost exceeded $1.4 billion in settlements and remediation.

5. Risk Analysis Methods

Risk analysis can be qualitative (descriptive categories like High/Medium/Low), quantitative (expressed in financial terms) or semi-quantitative (numerical scales mapped to categories).

Qualitative vs Quantitative Risk Analysis

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# QUALITATIVE RISK ANALYSIS
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
Risk Matrix (5×5)
  Likelihood:  1=Rare  2=Unlikely  3=Possible  4=Likely  5=Almost Certain
  Impact:      1=Negligible  2=Minor  3=Moderate  4=Major  5=Catastrophic
  Score:       Likelihood × Impact  →  1–25

Example:
  Threat:       Phishing email leads to credential theft
  Likelihood:  4 (Likely — employees receive phishing daily)
  Impact:       4 (Major — admin credential access)
  Score:        16 → HIGH RISK

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# QUANTITATIVE RISK ANALYSIS (ALE Method)
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
Key Terms:
  AV   # Asset Value — monetary value of the asset
  EF   # Exposure Factor — % of asset lost per incident (0.0–1.0)
  SLE  # Single Loss Expectancy = AV × EF
  ARO  # Annual Rate of Occurrence — expected incidents per year
  ALE  # Annual Loss Expectancy = SLE × ARO

Example:
  AV  = $500,000  # Customer database value
  EF  = 0.40      # 40% data exposed per breach
  SLE = $200,000  # AV × EF = cost per breach
  ARO = 0.25      # Once every 4 years
  ALE = $50,000   # SLE × ARO = annual expected loss

# Control cost decision:
# If control costs $30,000/year and reduces ALE to $10,000
# → Net benefit = $50,000 - $10,000 - $30,000 = $10,000/year ✅
Choosing the right method:

Qualitative — faster, requires less data, widely used for initial assessments and operational decisions. Suitable when precise financial data is unavailable
Quantitative (ALE) — produces financial figures that justify security investments to boards and CFOs. Requires reliable historical data, which is often scarce
Semi-quantitative — numerical scores on qualitative scales. Balances rigour with practicality — the most commonly used in industry

6. Security Control Types and Categories

⚠️
Controls must be matched to threats A detective control (e.g. SIEM alerting) cannot prevent a breach — it only discovers it after the fact. Effective security requires the right mix of preventive, detective and corrective controls for each risk.
Preventive
Control Function
Stop an attack before it occurs. Examples: firewalls, MFA, input validation, patch management, access controls.
Detective
Control Function
Identify attacks in progress or after the fact. Examples: SIEM, IDS, audit logs, anomaly detection, vulnerability scans.
Corrective
Control Function
Restore systems after an incident. Examples: backups, incident response plans, disaster recovery, patch deployment.
Deterrent
Control Function
Discourage attackers from attempting an attack. Examples: warning banners, CCTV signage, prosecution policies, bug bounty programmes.
Technical
Control Category
Implemented in hardware or software. Examples: encryption, antivirus, firewalls, access control lists, MFA.
Administrative
Control Category
Policies, procedures and training. Examples: security awareness training, background checks, acceptable use policies, incident response plans.

7. Defence in Depth — A Practical Example

Defence in Depth means structuring controls in concentric layers — like a castle with walls, moat, guards and a keep. If an attacker breaches one layer, they face another.

🌐 Perimeter
Firewall / WAF
→
🔗 Network
IDS / Segmentation
→
💻 Endpoint
EDR / Patching
→
🔑 Identity
MFA / Zero Trust
→
📦 Application
SAST / WAF rules
→
🗄️ Data
Encryption / DLP
→
👥 People
Awareness Training
  • ✓
    Layer controls so a failure at one level does not immediately compromise everything
  • ✓
    Use different technologies from different vendors at different layers — avoid single points of failure
  • ✓
    Include people and processes as layers — technology alone is not enough
  • !
    More layers increase complexity — ensure each layer is manageable and maintained
  • ✗
    Rely on perimeter security alone — once the firewall is breached, everything is exposed
  • ✗
    Neglect the "people" layer — the majority of breaches involve a human element

8. Risk Treatment, Tolerance and Communication

Once risks are identified and scored, the organisation must decide how to respond to each one. There are four fundamental treatment strategies, and the choice depends on cost, feasibility and risk appetite.

Risk Treatment Decision Framework

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# THE FOUR RISK TREATMENT OPTIONS
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────

1. MITIGATE (Reduce)
   Implement controls to reduce likelihood or impact
   When to use: Risk is above appetite; cost of control < cost of risk
   Example:     Deploy MFA to reduce credential theft likelihood

2. TRANSFER (Share)
   Shift financial consequences to a third party
   When to use: Risk impact is high but control cost is prohibitive
   Example:     Cyber insurance, outsourcing to an MSSP

3. AVOID (Eliminate)
   Remove the activity or asset that creates the risk
   When to use: Risk is unacceptable and cannot be reduced cost-effectively
   Example:     Discontinue collecting data you don't need

4. ACCEPT (Retain)
   Acknowledge and monitor the risk without additional action
   When to use: Risk is within appetite; control cost exceeds potential loss
   Requirement: Formal sign-off by an authorised risk owner + documentation

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# COMMUNICATING RISK TO LEADERSHIP
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
Wrong approach:
  "CVE-2024-1234 has a CVSS score of 9.8 in the
   libssl library affecting our RHEL 8 servers"

Right approach:
  "A critical vulnerability in our web servers could allow
   an external attacker to access customer payment data.
   Estimated impact: €2M fine + reputational damage.
   Patch available. Remediation cost: 2 engineer-days."
