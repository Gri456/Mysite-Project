🧠 Social Engineering & Human Security

Over 85% of data breaches involve a human element — not an unpatched server or a zero-day exploit. Attackers exploit cognitive biases, emotions and trust to bypass even the most sophisticated technical controls. A single well-crafted phishing email can render millions in security investment irrelevant.

In this module you will learn the psychology behind social engineering, the main attack techniques (phishing, vishing, pretexting, baiting, tailgating), how to design and run simulated phishing campaigns, and how to build an effective human security awareness programme.

🧠

1. The Psychology Behind Social Engineering

Social engineering does not exploit software — it exploits cognitive biases and psychological principles that are hardwired into human behaviour. Robert Cialdini's six principles of influence are the foundation of virtually every social engineering attack.

Cialdini's 6 Principles — How Attackers Use Them
⚡ Urgency & Scarcity
  • Creates time pressure so victims act before thinking
  • "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours"
  • "Act now — limited access window closing"
  • Bypasses rational evaluation by triggering anxiety
  • Most common trigger in phishing emails
🏛️ Authority
  • People comply with perceived authority figures
  • "This is the IT department — your password must be reset immediately"
  • Impersonating CEO, CISO, bank, tax authority, police
  • Uniform, title and confident tone amplify authority
  • BEC (Business Email Compromise) exploits this heavily
🤝 Social Proof & Liking
  • We do what others around us do (herd behaviour)
  • "Your colleague already approved this transfer"
  • Liking: people comply more with those they find likeable
  • Attackers build rapport over days/weeks before asking
  • Romance scams and LinkedIn attacks exploit liking
🎁 Reciprocity & Commitment
  • Reciprocity: we feel obliged to return favours
  • "I helped you last week — I just need this one thing"
  • Commitment: once we agree to small requests, we escalate
  • Foot-in-the-door technique starts with trivial asks
  • Long-con attacks build reciprocity systematically
⚠️
These biases are not weaknesses — they are features Urgency, trust in authority and social proof are adaptive human behaviours. Social engineering weaponises them. Training cannot eliminate these biases, but it can teach people to pause and verify when these triggers are activated.
🎭

2. Social Engineering Attack Taxonomy

Social engineering attacks span digital, voice and physical channels. Understanding each type — and how they chain together — is essential for both defence and simulated testing.

📧 Phishing (Email)
Mass or targeted fraudulent emails that trick recipients into clicking malicious links, downloading malware or submitting credentials. The most common initial access vector in breaches. Subtypes: spear phishing (targeted individual), whaling (targeting executives), clone phishing (duplicating a legitimate email).
📱 Smishing (SMS) & Vishing (Voice)
Smishing uses SMS/text messages to deliver malicious links or requests. Vishing uses phone calls — attackers impersonate banks, IT support, government agencies. AI voice cloning now makes vishing significantly more convincing, enabling attackers to synthesise the CEO's voice from public audio.
🎭 Pretexting
Creating a fabricated scenario (pretext) to extract information or access. Examples: posing as an IT technician needing remote access, a new employee needing onboarding help, or a vendor requiring payment details. Requires prior OSINT research to be believable.
💾 Baiting
Leaving physical media (USB drives, charging cables) or offering digital downloads that install malware when used. USB drop attacks in car parks remain highly effective — curiosity reliably overrides caution. In 2016, researchers dropped 300 USB drives on a university campus; 48% were plugged in within hours.
🚪 Tailgating & Piggybacking
Physically following an authorised person through a controlled access point without presenting credentials. Relies on social norms (people hold doors for each other). Tailgating is unaware (victim doesn't notice); piggybacking is aware (victim knowingly lets someone in).
🌐 Watering Hole Attacks
Compromising a website frequently visited by the target audience (e.g. an industry forum) rather than attacking targets directly. When targets visit the compromised site, malware is delivered. Named after predators waiting at watering holes in nature.
📧

3. Anatomy of a Phishing Email

Understanding how phishing emails are constructed is essential for both recognising attacks and designing effective simulated campaigns for awareness training.

Phishing Email — Annotated Structure

FROM:    it-support@company-helpdesk.net   # ← lookalike domain (not company.com)
TO:      john.smith@targetcompany.com
SUBJECT: URGENT: Your account will be disabled in 2 hours  # ← urgency trigger
DATE:    Friday 17:45  # ← sent at end-of-day to reduce scrutiny

────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Dear John,                                              # ← personalised (spear phish)

Our security team has detected unusual login activity  # ← fear trigger
on your account from IP 185.123.45.67 (Russia).       # ← false detail adds credibility

To protect your account, you must verify your
identity within 2 hours or access will be suspended.  # ← artificial deadline

[ VERIFY MY ACCOUNT NOW ]                              # ← button links to credential harvester

# Hover URL reveals: http://company-it-secure.net/login
# Legitimate URL would be: https://company.com/login

IT Security Team
CompanyName Help Desk                                   # ← mimics legitimate signature
Tel: +1 (555) 000-1234                                  # ← fake number adds legitimacy
Red flags to spot in every suspicious email:

Sender domain — Does it exactly match the organisation's real domain? Lookalikes: company-helpdesk.net vs company.com
Urgency + threat — Legitimate IT departments do not suspend accounts with 2-hour warnings via email
Hover before clicking — Always hover over links to preview the actual destination URL before clicking
Generic vs specific — Spear phishing uses your real name and role; mass phishing uses "Dear Customer"
Request type — Legitimate services never ask for passwords, MFA codes or payment details by email

4. Business Email Compromise (BEC) and Advanced Attacks

Business Email Compromise is the most financially damaging cybercrime category — costing organisations over $50 billion globally since 2013 according to the FBI. Unlike mass phishing, BEC is highly targeted, well-researched and often involves no malware — just social engineering.

💸 CEO Fraud
  • Attacker impersonates the CEO or CFO
  • Requests urgent wire transfer to a new account
  • "This is confidential — don't mention it to anyone"
  • Often timed around executive travel or M&A activity
  • Average loss per incident: $120,000+
🧾 Invoice Fraud
  • Compromise or spoof a legitimate supplier's email
  • Intercept an existing invoice thread
  • Change bank account details on the invoice
  • Payment goes to attacker's account
  • Victim doesn't know until supplier chases payment
🤖 AI Voice Cloning
  • Clone an executive's voice from public recordings
  • Call finance staff to authorise urgent transfers
  • In 2019: $243K stolen via cloned CEO voice (first confirmed case)
  • AI tools now need as little as 3 seconds of audio
  • Verify via callback to known number, not the caller's
💬 Deepfake Video
  • Synthesise video of executives in fake meetings
  • 2024: $25M stolen via deepfake video conference (Hong Kong)
  • Victim joined a multi-person fake Zoom call
  • Every "colleague" on the call was AI-generated
  • Verification: establish out-of-band code words for executives
📰 Real Case
Twitter Hack (2020) — Phone Vishing on IT Staff
Attackers called Twitter employees posing as IT support staff, convincing them to provide credentials to an internal admin tool. Once inside, they hijacked accounts belonging to Barack Obama, Elon Musk, Joe Biden and Apple — posting a Bitcoin scam that collected $120,000 in minutes. No technical exploit was used: just a phone call and a convincing pretext. Three teenagers were ultimately responsible.

5. Email Authentication — SPF, DKIM and DMARC

The primary technical defence against email spoofing is correct configuration of three DNS-based authentication standards. Without these, anyone can send email appearing to come from your domain.

SPF, DKIM and DMARC — Configuration Reference

# ── SPF (Sender Policy Framework) ──────────────────────────────
# DNS TXT record declaring which servers may send email for your domain
yourcompany.com. TXT
  "v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com include:mailchimp.com ip4:203.0.113.10 -all"
# -all = hard fail (reject), ~all = soft fail (mark as spam), +all = accept all (⚠️ never use)

# ── DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) ──────────────────────────
# Cryptographic signature added to outgoing emails, verified by receiver
selector._domainkey.yourcompany.com. TXT
  "v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=MIGfMA0GCSqGSIb3DQEBAQUAA4GNADCBiQKBgQC..."
# The private key signs outgoing mail; receivers verify with the public key above

# ── DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication) ─────────────────
# Policy: what to do when SPF/DKIM fail + reporting
_dmarc.yourcompany.com. TXT
  "v=DMARC1; p=reject; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourcompany.com; pct=100"

# p=none    → monitor only (start here)
# p=quarantine → send to spam
# p=reject  → block the email completely (goal state)

# ── Verify your configuration ───────────────────────────────────
dig TXT yourcompany.com          # check SPF
dig TXT _dmarc.yourcompany.com   # check DMARC
# Or use: https://mxtoolbox.com/EmailHeaders.aspx
Why all three are needed:

SPF alone — Can be bypassed; only checks the envelope sender, not the "From" header visible to users
DKIM alone — Proves integrity but doesn't prevent domain spoofing in the visible From header
DMARC — Enforces alignment between SPF/DKIM and the visible From domain, and specifies what to do when they don't match
p=reject — The gold standard: unauthorised emails claiming to be from your domain are rejected before reaching the inbox

6. Pretexting, Vishing and Physical Attacks

Physical and voice-based social engineering attacks are less common but often more impactful — they can bypass all digital security controls entirely.

Vishing Script Structure — IT Support Impersonation

# Attacker pre-call OSINT: LinkedIn → victim name, role, manager name
#                           Company website → IT support process
#                           Glassdoor → internal tool names

CALL STRUCTURE:

Opening (establish authority):
  "Hi Sarah, this is Mike from the IT Security team.
   I'm working with [manager name] on a security incident."

Create urgency:
  "We've detected unusual activity on your workstation
   and need to resolve this before the end of business today."

Make a small ask first (commitment):
  "Can you confirm your employee ID? Just to verify I have
   the right account."  # harmless — foot in the door

Escalate the ask:
  "I need you to open a browser and go to this support URL
   so I can push a security patch remotely."
  # URL installs RAT (remote access trojan)

VICTIM DEFENCE — how to respond:
  STOP:   Never give credentials, install software or visit
           URLs from unsolicited callers, regardless of who they claim to be
  VERIFY: Hang up. Call IT support back on the official number
           from the company intranet — not a number the caller provides
  REPORT: Report the call to your security team immediately
  • ✓
    Verify all unexpected IT requests by calling back on a known, official number
  • ✓
    Challenge all visitors at reception — even those wearing uniforms or carrying equipment
  • ✓
    Never allow tailgating through badge-controlled doors, even to "help" someone with their hands full
  • ✓
    Report lost or found USB drives to IT immediately — never plug them in
  • !
    Be especially vigilant on Fridays and public holiday eves — attackers exploit reduced-scrutiny periods
  • ✗
    Do not share internal information (tool names, project names, org structure) on LinkedIn or social media

GoPhish — Campaign Setup Reference

# Install GoPhish (open-source phishing simulation)
wget https://github.com/gophish/gophish/releases/latest/gophish.zip
unzip gophish.zip && ./gophish
# Admin panel: https://localhost:3333 (default)

CAMPAIGN COMPONENTS IN GOPHISH:

1. Sending Profile
   SMTP server, from name, from address
   # Use a domain similar to but not impersonating your company
   # Set up SPF/DKIM on the sending domain to avoid spam filters

2. Email Template
   Subject, HTML body, attachments
   Use {{.FirstName}} {{.LastName}} for personalisation
   Track: {{.Tracker}} (invisible pixel)
   Link: {{.URL}} (your landing page)

3. Landing Page
   Clone target login page or create credential harvester
   Redirect to training page after submission
   # Best practice: redirect to awareness training, not just a "gotcha" page

4. Target Group
   CSV import: First Name, Last Name, Email, Position

KEY METRICS TO TRACK:
  Email Open Rate     # % who opened the email
  Click Rate          # % who clicked the link (primary KPI)
  Submission Rate     # % who entered credentials
  Report Rate         # % who reported to security team (often ignored — track this!)
🚫
Ethical guardrails for simulated campaigns Never name and shame employees who click. Never use emotionally manipulative pretexts (fake job loss, fake family emergency). Always disclose to HR and legal before running. Use results for education, not punishment. The goal is behaviour change, not blame.

8. Building an Effective Security Awareness Programme

A one-time annual compliance training is largely ineffective — research shows retention drops to near zero within weeks. Effective security awareness is continuous, contextual and behaviour-focused.

❌ What Doesn't Work
  • Annual 2-hour compliance click-through courses
  • Generic "don't click links" posters
  • Fear-based messaging without empowerment
  • Blaming users when they fall for phishing
  • Measuring completion rate instead of behaviour change
✅ What Works
  • Monthly bite-sized micro-training (5 min modules)
  • Just-in-time training triggered by clicking simulated phish
  • Positive reinforcement: reward reporters, not just catching clickers
  • Real incident examples relevant to your industry
  • Track phishing click rates over 12+ months
📊 Key Metrics
  • Phishing Simulation Click Rate (target: <5%)
  • Phishing Report Rate (employees alerting security)
  • Mean time to report a suspicious email
  • Repeat clickers (high-risk individuals)
  • Department-level risk scoring
🔧 Technical Controls (Complements Training)
  • MFA on all accounts — mitigates credential theft
  • DMARC p=reject on all owned domains
  • Email banners for external senders
  • Browser URL/link sandboxing
  • Disable macros in Office documents by default
📰 Real Case
MGM Resorts Breach (2023) — 10-Minute Vishing Call
Attackers found an MGM employee on LinkedIn, then called the IT helpdesk impersonating that employee to reset their Okta MFA. The entire attack — from LinkedIn research to active session — took approximately 10 minutes. The breach disrupted MGM operations for 10+ days and cost an estimated $100 million. The Scattered Spider group behind the attack was known for targeting IT helpdesks specifically. MGM had no strict callback verification protocol for MFA resets.
