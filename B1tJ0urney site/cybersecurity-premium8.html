🏛️ GRC Fundamentals in Cybersecurity

GRC stands for Governance, Risk Management and Compliance — the three pillars that allow an organisation to align its security strategy with business objectives, manage threats systematically and meet legal and regulatory requirements.

In this module you will learn the GRC model, the main international frameworks (ISO 27001, NIST, SOC 2, GDPR), how to conduct risk assessments, write effective security policies and prepare for audits.

🏛️

1. What is GRC?

GRC is an integrated approach that helps organisations achieve their objectives while managing uncertainty and acting with integrity. Rather than treating governance, risk and compliance as separate silos, GRC unifies them into a coherent strategy — ensuring that security decisions are driven by business context and measurable outcomes.

⚠️
Why GRC matters Organisations without a formal GRC programme face an average data breach cost of $4.88 million (IBM, 2024). Regulatory fines under GDPR alone have exceeded €4.5 billion since enforcement began.

2. Security Governance

Security governance defines the structures, responsibilities and processes by which an organisation directs and controls its information security programme. It answers the question: "Who is accountable for security decisions and how are they made?"

Security Policy Framework
A hierarchy of documents: top-level Information Security Policy → topic-specific standards (e.g. access control, encryption) → operational procedures → work instructions. Each level provides increasingly specific guidance.
Roles and Responsibilities — RACI
Clearly define who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted and Informed for each security activity. The CISO owns the programme; the Board provides oversight; all employees have a role in execution.
Security Steering Committee
A cross-functional body (IT, Legal, HR, Finance, Operations) that reviews security priorities, approves risk acceptance decisions and ensures security investments align with business strategy.
KPIs and Security Metrics
Measure what matters: mean time to detect (MTTD), mean time to respond (MTTR), patch compliance rate, phishing simulation click rates, percentage of assets with known vulnerabilities. Report to leadership in business terms.

3. Risk Management Frameworks

Risk management is the systematic process of identifying, analysing, evaluating and treating threats to organisational assets. Multiple internationally recognised frameworks exist — each with its own scope and strengths.

ISO/IEC 27001
  • International standard for Information Security Management Systems (ISMS)
  • Risk-based approach with 93 controls in Annex A
  • Certifiable — third-party audit required
  • Scope: global, all sectors and organisation sizes
  • 🌍 Most widely adopted security standard worldwide
NIST Cybersecurity Framework
  • Five functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
  • Flexible — adapts to any organisation size or sector
  • Version 2.0 adds Govern as a sixth function (2024)
  • Widely used in US critical infrastructure and government
  • 🇺🇸 Foundational framework for US federal agencies
SOC 2 (Type I & Type II)
  • Auditing standard for service organisations (AICPA)
  • Five Trust Service Criteria: Security, Availability, Confidentiality, Processing Integrity, Privacy
  • Type I: controls exist at a point in time
  • Type II: controls effective over 6–12 months
  • ☁️ Common requirement for SaaS and cloud providers
GDPR & Data Protection
  • EU regulation on personal data processing and privacy
  • Key principles: lawfulness, purpose limitation, data minimisation
  • Breach notification within 72 hours to supervisory authority
  • Fines up to €20M or 4% of global annual turnover
  • 🇪🇺 Applies to any entity processing EU residents' data

4. Risk Assessment — Step by Step

Risk Assessment Methodology (ISO 27005 / NIST SP 800-30)

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
#  STEP 1 — Asset Identification
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
Asset:       Customer Database (PostgreSQL, prod)
Owner:       Head of Engineering
CIA Rating:  Confidentiality=HIGH, Integrity=HIGH, Availability=MEDIUM

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
#  STEP 2 — Threat & Vulnerability Identification
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
Threat:      SQL Injection attack by external actor
Vuln:        Unsanitised user input in login endpoint
Threat:      Insider data exfiltration
Vuln:        Excessive DB read privileges for dev team

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
#  STEP 3 — Risk Calculation
#  Risk = Likelihood × Impact
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
SQL Injection:
  Likelihood:  4/5  (known CVE, exposed endpoint)
  Impact:      5/5  (full data exposure)
  Risk Score:  20/25 → CRITICAL

Insider Threat:
  Likelihood:  2/5  (small dev team, background-checked)
  Impact:      5/5  (full data exposure)
  Risk Score:  10/25 → HIGH

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
#  STEP 4 — Risk Treatment Options
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────
MITIGATE:   Implement parameterised queries, WAF rules
TRANSFER:   Cyber insurance policy covering data breach
ACCEPT:     Residual risk approved by CISO + documented
AVOID:      Remove feature / discontinue data collection
Key concepts explained:

CIA Triad — Every asset is rated on Confidentiality, Integrity and Availability to determine its business criticality
Risk = Likelihood × Impact — The standard formula used in qualitative and semi-quantitative risk assessments
Risk Treatment — Four options: Mitigate (reduce), Transfer (insure/outsource), Accept (document and monitor), Avoid (eliminate)
Residual Risk — The risk that remains after controls are applied; must be formally accepted by the risk owner

5. Writing Effective Security Policies

🚫
Common policy failure modes Policies that are too vague to enforce, never reviewed after creation, unknown to employees, or written without input from the business are worse than no policy — they create false assurance and fail audits.

Policy Document Structure — Template

INFORMATION SECURITY POLICY
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Document ID:   ISP-001
Version:       2.1
Owner:         Chief Information Security Officer
Review Date:   Annual (next: 2025-12-01)
Classification: Internal

1. PURPOSE
   Define the organisation's intent to protect
   information assets from all threats.

2. SCOPE
   All employees, contractors, systems and data
   owned or processed by [Organisation].

3. POLICY STATEMENTS
   3.1 All access must follow least privilege principle
   3.2 MFA required for all remote access and admin accounts
   3.3 Data classified as Confidential must be encrypted at rest
   3.4 Security incidents must be reported within 4 hours

4. EXCEPTIONS
   Must be formally approved by CISO, documented
   with business justification and time-bounded.

5. ENFORCEMENT
   Violations may result in disciplinary action
   up to and including termination or legal action.

6. RELATED DOCUMENTS
   Access Control Standard (ACS-001)
   Incident Response Plan (IRP-002)
   Acceptable Use Policy (AUP-003)
  • ✓
    Keep policies short and clear — employees must be able to understand and follow them
  • ✓
    Review and update policies at least annually or after significant changes
  • ✓
    Obtain formal sign-off from senior management to give policies authority
  • !
    Train all employees on relevant policies — awareness is a compliance requirement in ISO 27001
  • ✗
    Write policies that can never be audited or enforced — vague language defeats the purpose
  • ✗
    Create policies without exceptions processes — rigid policies breed shadow IT and workarounds

6. Compliance Programmes and Audit Readiness

Compliance means demonstrating that controls are in place and effective — not just that policies exist. Audit readiness is an ongoing state, not something you prepare for in the weeks before an audit.

ISO 27001 — Control Domains (Annex A)

# ISO/IEC 27001:2022 — 4 Control Themes, 93 Controls

ORGANISATIONAL CONTROLS (37 controls)
  5.1  Policies for information security
  5.9  Inventory of information and assets
  5.23 Information security for use of cloud services
  5.30 ICT readiness for business continuity

PEOPLE CONTROLS (8 controls)
  6.3  Information security awareness, education & training
  6.4  Disciplinary process for security violations
  6.6  Confidentiality or non-disclosure agreements

PHYSICAL CONTROLS (14 controls)
  7.1  Physical security perimeters
  7.4  Physical security monitoring (CCTV, access logs)
  7.9  Security of assets off-premises (remote work)

TECHNOLOGICAL CONTROLS (34 controls)
  8.2  Privileged access rights management
  8.7  Protection against malware
  8.16 Monitoring activities (logs, SIEM)
  8.24 Use of cryptography
  8.28 Secure coding practices
Audit evidence types:

Document evidence — Policies, procedures, contracts, risk registers, training records
Technical evidence — Configuration screenshots, log exports, vulnerability scan reports, penetration test results
Interview evidence — Auditors speak with staff to verify controls are understood and practiced
Observation evidence — Auditors watch processes being performed (e.g. onboarding, incident response drills)

7. Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM)

Organisations increasingly depend on suppliers, cloud providers and contractors who may have access to sensitive data or critical systems. TPRM ensures that third-party risk is assessed, monitored and contractually managed throughout the relationship lifecycle.

📰 Real Case
Target Data Breach (2013) — Third-Party Vector
Attackers gained entry to Target's network through a stolen HVAC vendor credential. The breach exposed 40 million credit card records and cost over $300 million in settlements. The root cause was inadequate third-party access controls and network segmentation — a TPRM failure that drove major changes to how organisations vet and monitor suppliers.

TPRM Lifecycle

PHASE 1 — Due Diligence (before onboarding)
  ▸ Classify vendor by data access and criticality (Tier 1/2/3)
  ▸ Request security questionnaire (SIG, CAIQ, custom)
  ▸ Review SOC 2 report, ISO 27001 certificate, pen test summary
  ▸ Check for sanctions, legal history, financial stability

PHASE 2 — Contractual Controls
  ▸ Data Processing Agreement (DPA) — required under GDPR
  ▸ Right to audit clause in contract
  ▸ Incident notification SLA (e.g. within 24/48 hours)
  ▸ Subprocessor restrictions and approval process

PHASE 3 — Ongoing Monitoring
  ▸ Annual reassessment for Tier 1 vendors
  ▸ Continuous monitoring via BitSight / SecurityScorecard
  ▸ Review breach notifications and CVE disclosures
  ▸ Offboarding: revoke access, delete data, close accounts
✅
TPRM best practices Tier vendors by risk (critical/high/medium/low) • Never grant more access than the vendor needs (least privilege) • Use a standardised questionnaire (e.g. CAIQ from CSA) • Require breach notification clauses in all contracts • Monitor suppliers continuously with attack surface intelligence tools

8. Business Continuity & Incident Response in GRC

GRC programmes must include plans for when controls fail. Business Continuity Planning (BCP) and Incident Response (IR) are governance artefacts as much as operational ones — they must be documented, tested and linked to risk treatment decisions.

Key Metrics: RTO, RPO and BIA

# Business Impact Analysis (BIA) — Critical Definitions

RTO — Recovery Time Objective
  Definition:  Maximum acceptable downtime after a disruption
  Example:     Payment system RTO = 4 hours
  Impact:      Drives backup infrastructure and failover investment

RPO — Recovery Point Objective
  Definition:  Maximum acceptable data loss (measured in time)
  Example:     Database RPO = 1 hour → backups every 60 minutes
  Impact:      Drives backup frequency and replication strategy

MTD — Maximum Tolerable Downtime
  Definition:  Point beyond which the organisation cannot survive
  Example:     Core banking MTD = 24 hours

# Incident Severity Classification (GRC perspective)
SEV-1 CRITICAL:  Breach confirmed, regulatory notification triggered
SEV-2 HIGH:      Suspected breach, containment in progress
SEV-3 MEDIUM:    Policy violation, no data exposure confirmed
SEV-4 LOW:       Minor event, logged and monitored

# GDPR Breach Notification Timeline
  72 hours → Notify supervisory authority (e.g. ICO, CNPD)
  Without undue delay → Notify affected data subjects if high risk
