Introduction: Why Ethics Matters in AI

AI has become a core part of modern society, influencing:

  • Credit decisions
  • Medical diagnoses
  • Hiring and HR
  • Law enforcement
  • Education
  • Social media
  • Public opinion

Because AI systems affect real lives, ethical design is essential. Ethical AI aims to ensure systems that are:

  • Fair
  • Transparent
  • Accountable
  • Respectful of privacy
  • Safe and socially beneficial

This module explains the principles and challenges behind responsible AI.

The core ethical principles — what they mean in practice:

⚖️
Fairness
AI systems must not discriminate or disadvantage groups based on protected characteristics (race, gender, age, disability). This means equitable outcomes across populations — not just equal treatment.
🔍
Transparency
Users and stakeholders should understand how AI systems work and how decisions are made. This includes model cards, datasheets, and explainability tools that make the "why" visible.
🛡️
Accountability
There must always be a human or organization responsible for an AI system's actions and consequences. Algorithmic decisions should not shield institutions from responsibility.
🔒
Privacy by Design
Privacy protections should be built into AI systems from the beginning — not added as an afterthought. This includes data minimization, access controls, and consent management.
🌍
Beneficence & Non-Maleficence
AI should actively benefit society and avoid causing harm. This requires careful risk assessment before deployment, particularly in high-stakes domains like healthcare, justice, and finance.
📌 The Stakes of Getting Ethics Wrong

In 2019, a healthcare AI used in US hospitals was found to systematically allocate fewer care resources to Black patients than equally sick white patients — because it used healthcare costs as a proxy for healthcare needs, reflecting existing systemic inequality. Approximately 760,000 people were affected. This is a canonical example of why intent ≠ fairness: the algorithm was not designed to be biased, but its training objective encoded existing injustice.

Algorithmic Bias – When AI Is Unfair

Bias occurs when AI models make decisions that systematically disadvantage certain groups.

Bias can emerge from:

  • Training data
  • Model architecture
  • Social inequalities embedded in data
  • Human annotation errors
  • Lack of diversity in datasets

Examples of AI bias:

  • Facial recognition misidentifying people with darker skin
  • Job screening algorithms favoring men
  • Credit scoring systems discriminating against minority groups
  • Medical tools underdiagnosing certain populations

Types of bias:

  • Prejudicial bias → rooted in social/institutional inequality
  • Statistical bias → incorrect assumptions
  • Selection bias → unrepresentative data
  • Measurement bias → flawed labeling

Unbiased AI is a myth — but mitigated AI is possible.

Real-world cases and the full taxonomy of bias sources:

HIGH IMPACT
Case Study
COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm
Used in US courts to predict re-offending risk. ProPublica found it incorrectly flagged Black defendants as future criminals at nearly twice the rate of white defendants. Training data reflected policing patterns — not actual offending rates.
HIGH IMPACT
Case Study
Amazon's CV Screening Tool
Built to automate hiring, the model penalized CVs that included the word "women's" (e.g. women's chess club) and downgraded graduates from all-women's colleges. It learned from 10 years of historical hiring patterns dominated by male engineers.
MEDIUM IMPACT
Case Study
Face Recognition Failures
MIT Media Lab's "Gender Shades" study found commercial face recognition systems had error rates of up to 35% for darker-skinned women vs 1% for lighter-skinned men. The training data was predominantly lighter-skinned faces.
MEDIUM IMPACT
Case Study
Word Embeddings & Stereotype
Google's word2vec embeddings placed "man" closer to "computer programmer" and "woman" closer to "homemaker." NLP models trained on this data would reproduce these stereotypes in downstream applications — job recommendations, resume scoring, language generation.

The complete bias taxonomy — where bias can enter the ML pipeline:

Strategies to Reduce Bias

✔ Better datasets
Diverse, representative, well-labeled data.

✔ Bias detection tools
Fairness metrics, distribution analysis, model audits.

✔ Transparent design
Explainable systems that reveal decision logic.

✔ Human oversight
Critical decisions must include humans-in-the-loop.

✔ Regular evaluation
Models must be retrained as society evolves.

The technical toolkit for bias detection and mitigation:

Pre-Processing
Fix the Data Before Training
Reweighting: assign higher importance to underrepresented samples. Resampling: oversample minority groups (SMOTE). Relabeling: remove noisy labels. Disparate impact analysis: check if any feature acts as a proxy for protected attributes.
In-Processing
Build Fairness Into Training
Adversarial debiasing: train an adversary to predict protected attributes from the model's internal representations, then penalize it. Constraint-based training: add fairness constraints (demographic parity, equalized odds) directly to the loss function.
Post-Processing
Adjust Outputs After Training
Threshold adjustment: set different decision thresholds per demographic group to equalize false positive rates. Calibration: ensure confidence scores are equally calibrated across groups. Reject option classification: abstain from prediction when confidence is low for certain groups.
Fairness Metrics
Measuring What "Fair" Means
Demographic Parity: equal positive prediction rates across groups. Equalized Odds: equal true and false positive rates. Individual Fairness: similar individuals receive similar predictions. Counterfactual Fairness: prediction doesn't change if protected attribute changes. Note: not all metrics can be simultaneously satisfied.
⚠️ The Impossibility of Perfect Fairness

Chouldechova (2017) proved mathematically that several common fairness criteria are mutually incompatible when base rates differ between groups. You cannot simultaneously achieve calibration (predicted scores are accurate), equal false positive rates, and equal false negative rates. This means fairness in AI is ultimately a values choice, not a purely technical problem — organizations must decide which notion of fairness is most appropriate for each context.

Privacy in AI – Protecting User Data

AI models often rely on large amounts of data. If not handled correctly, it leads to privacy violations.

Common risks:

  • Unauthorized data collection
  • Leaking personal information
  • Re-identification attacks
  • Mishandling sensitive data (health, location, identity)

Key privacy principles:

  • Data minimization → collect only what is needed
  • Encryption → secure storage and transmission
  • Anonymization → remove personally identifiable information
  • Consent → users must agree to data usage
  • Right to be forgotten → delete user data on request

Regulations: GDPR (Europe) · CCPA (California)

Privacy is not optional — it is legally required.

Advanced privacy risks specific to AI — and technical defenses:

AI-Specific Risk
Membership Inference Attacks
An attacker can query a model to determine whether a specific individual's data was in the training set. This can reveal sensitive membership (e.g., "was this person's medical record used to train this disease model?"). Defense: differential privacy.
AI-Specific Risk
Model Inversion Attacks
By repeatedly querying a model, attackers can reconstruct training data — including faces from facial recognition models. Demonstrated successfully against commercial models. Defense: output perturbation, strict query limits.
Technical Defense
Differential Privacy (DP)
Add carefully calibrated mathematical noise to training data or model outputs, so individual records can't be inferred. Used by Apple (keyboard predictions), Google (Chrome telemetry), and the US Census Bureau. Tradeoff: some accuracy is lost for privacy guarantees.
Technical Defense
Federated Learning
Train models on-device across millions of phones without the raw data ever leaving the device. Only model updates (gradients) are sent to a central server and aggregated. Used by Google for Gboard, Apple for Siri. Raw data never leaves the user's device.
📋 GDPR Key Rights — What Developers Must Implement

Right to Explanation (Article 22) — Users subject to automated decisions have the right to a meaningful explanation. This directly requires explainability in AI systems.
Right to Erasure ("Right to be Forgotten") — Users can request deletion of their data. For ML models, this may require unlearning or model retraining.
Data Portability — Users can request their data in a machine-readable format.
Purpose Limitation — Data collected for one purpose cannot be used for another without explicit consent. Re-using training data for new models may violate this.
Penalties — Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher.

Transparency & Explainability

Black-box AI systems (like deep learning) are powerful but hard to understand. Explainability is crucial when:

  • Decisions affect people (loans, jobs, healthcare)
  • Regulators require justification
  • Users need confidence in AI tools

Examples of explainability tools:

  • SHAP
  • LIME
  • Attention heatmaps
  • Model cards
  • Data provenance reports

Explainable AI = trustworthy AI.

How XAI tools work — and when to use each:

ToolHow It WorksBest ForLimitation
SHAP
(SHapley Additive exPlanations)		Assigns each feature a Shapley value — its contribution to a specific prediction, averaged across all possible feature orderingsTabular data, credit scoring, feature importanceComputationally expensive for large datasets
LIME
(Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic)		Fits a simple interpretable model (linear regression) to approximate the complex model's behavior locally around a single predictionText and image classification, any modelExplanations can be unstable; sensitive to perturbation
GradCAM
(Gradient-weighted Class Activation)		Uses gradient information to highlight which image regions most influenced a CNN's prediction — produces a visual heatmapImage classification, medical imagingOnly works for CNNs; heatmaps can be misleading
Counterfactual Explanations"What is the minimum change to the input that would change the output?" — e.g., "Your loan was denied; if your income were €5,000 higher, it would have been approved"Regulatory compliance, user-facing explanationsMay suggest impractical changes; multiple counterfactuals exist
Model CardsStandardized documentation describing a model's purpose, training data, performance metrics across subgroups, intended use, limitations, and ethical considerationsModel release, procurement, auditingRequires organizational commitment to maintain honestly
💡 Interpretability vs Explainability vs Transparency

Interpretability — Can a human understand the model's mechanism directly? Decision trees and linear models are inherently interpretable. Neural networks are not.
Explainability — Can we generate post-hoc explanations for a model's specific predictions? (SHAP, LIME, GradCAM). These approximate but don't fully capture what the model actually does.
Transparency — Is information about the model, data, training process, and limitations publicly available? (Model cards, datasheets, audit reports).
All three matter — and they serve different audiences: engineers, users, and regulators.

Accountability – Who Is Responsible When AI Fails?

If an AI causes harm, responsibility may fall on:

  • Developers
  • Companies
  • Deployers
  • Data providers
  • Auditors

Key principle:
"AI systems should never remove human accountability." This ensures humans remain responsible for decisions, not algorithms.

The accountability gap — and how to close it:

Problem
The Accountability Gap
When AI causes harm, responsibility is often diffused: developers claim they didn't know how the system would be used; deployers claim the developer should have prevented misuse; regulators lack technical expertise; victims lack legal standing to challenge algorithmic decisions.
Framework
AI Impact Assessments
Before deploying high-risk AI, organizations should conduct structured risk assessments: Who could be harmed? What are the failure modes? What safeguards exist? Who reviews outputs? Who do affected parties contact? The EU AI Act mandates this for high-risk systems.
Tool
Audit Trails & Logging
Production AI systems should log inputs, outputs, model versions, and decision timestamps. This enables post-hoc investigation when something goes wrong, supports regulatory compliance, and allows identification of systematic errors in deployed models.
Principle
Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)
For high-stakes decisions (medical diagnosis, bail, benefits), a human should review AI outputs before action is taken. HITL is not just ethics — it's increasingly a regulatory requirement. The EU AI Act prohibits fully automated high-risk decisions without human oversight.

Social Impact – How AI Changes Society

AI brings positive impacts:

  • Better medical diagnoses
  • More efficient transportation
  • Personalized learning
  • Accessible digital tools
  • Faster scientific research

But also negative risks:

  • Job displacement
  • Manipulation of public opinion
  • Deepfakes
  • Increased inequality
  • Surveillance misuse

Ethical AI design must balance innovation with protection.

The structural impacts of AI on labor, power, and inequality:

Labor Automation — McKinsey estimates 400 million to 800 million jobs could be displaced by automation by 2030. Most at risk: routine cognitive tasks (data entry, basic customer service, paralegal work). Least at risk: creative work, complex human interaction, and skilled trades requiring dexterity. Key concern: job displacement will not be geographically or demographically even — it will hit developing economies and lower-income workers hardest.

Power Concentration — The most powerful AI systems require billions of dollars in compute, vast proprietary datasets, and thousands of engineers. This creates barriers to entry that concentrate AI power in 5–10 companies globally. Small businesses, developing nations, and civil society organizations cannot build or audit these systems — they can only consume them. This raises concerns about AI sovereignty and dependency.

Environmental Cost — Training a large language model produces significant CO₂ emissions. GPT-3's training was estimated to consume ~1,287 MWh of electricity and emit ~500 metric tons of CO₂. AI inference at scale (billions of daily queries) compounds this. Data centers currently use ~200 TWh/year globally — comparable to some small nations. Sustainable AI requires energy-efficient architectures, hardware advances, and renewable energy commitments.

AI and Democracy — Recommendation algorithms on social media maximize engagement — which is correlated with emotional arousal, outrage, and polarization. Studies show algorithmic amplification has deepened political polarization. AI-generated political content, synthetic media in campaigns, and microtargeted political ads raise fundamental questions about democratic discourse and informed consent in elections.

Deepfakes & Disinformation

Generative AI can create:

  • Fake videos
  • Synthetic voices
  • Manipulated evidence
  • Realistic fake identities

Threats:

  • Political misinformation
  • Fraud/scams
  • Reputation damage

Solutions:

  • Watermarking AI content
  • Detection tools
  • Regulations
  • User education

The technical arms race between deepfake creation and detection:

Attack
How Deepfakes Are Made
Face-swapping models (GAN-based or diffusion-based) replace one person's face with another's in video, synchronizing lighting, skin tone, and facial motion. Voice cloning models (ElevenLabs, VALL-E) can clone a voice from 3 seconds of audio. Full synthetic video of a person speaking can now be generated from just a photo and a text script.
Defense
C2PA Provenance Standards
The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA), backed by Adobe, Microsoft, and camera manufacturers, embeds cryptographic metadata into media files at creation time. This creates a verifiable chain of custody. Content Credentials show who created the media, when, and with what tool — even after the image is edited.
Defense
AI Watermarking
Google DeepMind's SynthID embeds imperceptible watermarks into AI-generated images and audio. The watermark survives compression, cropping, and filters. Meta's Stable Signature embeds watermarks at the diffusion model decoder level. EU AI Act mandates disclosure labels on AI-generated synthetic media.
Risk
The Liar's Dividend
As deepfakes become more prevalent, a paradox emerges: real evidence can now be dismissed as "probably AI-generated." Politicians and public figures can deny authentic videos are real. This "liar's dividend" may be more dangerous than deepfakes themselves — eroding trust in all media, including genuine evidence of wrongdoing.

AI Governance & Regulation

Governments and institutions are creating frameworks to ensure safe AI.

Examples:

  • EU AI Act
  • IEEE ethical standards
  • OECD guidelines
  • NIST AI risk management framework

Key pillars: Safety · Fairness · Transparency · Accountability · Robustness

Global AI governance landscape — what each major framework actually requires:

FrameworkJurisdictionApproachKey Requirements
EU AI Act (2024) European Union Risk-based classification Unacceptable risk: banned (social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance). High risk: conformity assessments, human oversight, audit trails. GPAI: transparency, copyright compliance. Penalties: up to €35M or 7% of revenue.
US Executive Order on AI (2023) United States Agency-based guidance Mandatory safety testing for frontier AI models before release. Disclosure requirements for AI-generated content. Standards for watermarking. No single comprehensive law — sector-specific guidance.
China AI Regulations (2023) China Service-focused rules Generative AI services must register with authorities, label AI content, prevent content that "undermines social stability," and use training data with lawful provenance.
NIST AI RMF United States (voluntary) Risk management framework Four core functions: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage. Provides organizations with a structured process for identifying, evaluating, and managing AI risks throughout the system lifecycle.
OECD AI Principles International (38 countries) Soft law / principles Five principles: Inclusive growth, human-centered values, transparency, robustness/security, accountability. Non-binding but widely adopted as reference for national regulation.
🏛️ The EU AI Act Risk Classification in Practice

Unacceptable Risk (Banned): Social credit scoring, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, emotional recognition at work/schools, predictive policing of individuals.
High Risk (Strict Rules): Hiring/CV screening tools, credit scoring, educational assessment, healthcare triage, criminal justice, migration management, critical infrastructure.
Limited Risk (Transparency Required): Chatbots (must disclose they're AI), deepfakes (must label), emotion recognition tools.
Minimal Risk (No Requirements): Spam filters, AI in video games, recommendation systems.
